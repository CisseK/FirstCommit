Kubernetes for people who've never heard of it

by Ayush  ·  5 min read

I spent three weeks thinking Kubernetes was something only senior engineers with years of experience could understand. Then I read one good explanation and it clicked in about ten minutes. Here's that explanation.

The problem Kubernetes solves

Let's say you've built a web app and you're running it as a Docker container on a single server. Things are going well. Then traffic spikes — suddenly you need five instances of your app running instead of one. Or your server crashes at 2am and your app goes down with it. Or you need to deploy a new version without taking the whole thing offline.

These are real problems every team running containers at scale hits. And solving them manually — SSH-ing into servers, starting containers by hand, watching for crashes — doesn't scale. That's what Kubernetes is for.

What Kubernetes actually does

Kubernetes is a system for managing containers across a group of machines. You tell it what you want — "I want three copies of this container running at all times, and if one crashes, restart it" — and Kubernetes figures out how to make that happen and keeps it that way.

The key word is desired state. You declare the state you want (in a YAML file), and Kubernetes constantly works to match reality to that declaration. If a container dies, Kubernetes starts a new one. If a node (server) goes down, Kubernetes moves its containers to a healthy node.

A few terms you'll hear constantly

Once you understand these four, you can follow most Kubernetes conversations.

  • Cluster — the whole system. A cluster is a set of machines (called nodes) that Kubernetes manages together.
  • Node — one of those machines. Could be a VM, a physical server, or a cloud instance. The cluster has a control plane node (the "brain") and worker nodes (where your apps actually run).
  • Pod — the smallest unit Kubernetes knows about. A pod wraps one or more containers that need to work together. In practice, most pods have just one container.
  • Deployment — a higher-level object that manages pods. You tell the Deployment "I want 3 replicas of this pod" and it creates and manages those pods for you. When you update your app, you update the Deployment.

Why K8s and not Kubernetes?

There are 8 letters between the K and the s in "Kubernetes." So people write K8s for short. Same reason i18n means "internationalization" and a11y means "accessibility." It's a developer shorthand thing.

Do you actually need Kubernetes?

Honest answer: probably not yet. Kubernetes is genuinely complex, and it's only worth that complexity at a certain scale. If you're running a small app on one server, Docker Compose is probably enough.

But understanding what Kubernetes does — even at this high level — makes you a much better collaborator on any team that does run it. And in DevOps and cloud native, that's almost every team.
